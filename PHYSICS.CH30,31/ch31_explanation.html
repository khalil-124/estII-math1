1. Ionizing Radiation

Radiation that has enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, creating ions.

Absorbed Dose

The absorbed dose measures the energy deposited per unit mass of material.

\[\text{Absorbed Dose} = \frac{\text{Energy Absorbed}}{\text{Mass}}\]

Units:

  • rad: 1 rad = 0.01 J/kg = 10‚Åª¬≤ J/kg
  • Gray (Gy): 1 Gy = 1 J/kg = 100 rad (SI unit)

‚úì Quick Check: How many rad equal 1 Gray?

Answer: C (1 Gy = 100 rad)

Effective Dose & Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE)

Different types of radiation cause different amounts of biological damage. The RBE (or Quality Factor, QF) accounts for this.

Radiation Type RBE (Quality Factor)
X-rays, Gamma rays, Beta 1
Slow Neutrons 3-5
Fast Neutrons, Protons 10
Alpha particles 10-20
\[\text{Effective Dose} = \text{Absorbed Dose} \times \text{RBE}\]

Units:

  • If dose in rad ‚Üí Effective dose in rem
  • If dose in Gray ‚Üí Effective dose in Sievert (Sv)

3. Radiation Exposure Limits

Natural Background Radiation: Approximately 0.3 rem/year (3 mSv/year) from cosmic rays, radon, and natural radioactive materials.

Occupational Limits for Radiation Workers

  • Maximum: 5 rem (50 mSv) in any single year
  • Average: Below 2 rem/year over 5 years
‚ö†Ô∏è Dangerous Dose Levels:
  • 400 rem (4 Sv): ~50% fatality rate (LD50)
  • 1000 rem (10 Sv): Almost always fatal

4. Medical Applications of Radiation

Radiation Therapy

Cancer cells can be destroyed using targeted radiation. To minimize damage to healthy tissue:

  • The radiation source is often rotated around the patient
  • Beams converge on the tumor from multiple angles
  • Surrounding tissues receive lower doses

PET Scan (Positron Emission Tomography)

Uses positron-emitting isotopes (like F-18) to create 3D images of metabolic processes in the body.

SPECT (Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography)

Uses gamma-emitting isotopes to create 3D images, commonly used for heart and brain imaging.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

Uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves (NOT ionizing radiation) to create detailed images of soft tissues.

Key Difference: MRI does NOT use ionizing radiation, making it safer for repeated imaging.

‚≠ê Level 1: Basic Conversion

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: Energy Calculation

\[\text{Energy} = \text{Dose} \times \text{Mass} = 0.5 \times 70 = \boxed{35 \text{ J}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Local Dose

A tumor (mass 50g) absorbs 0.15 J of radiation energy. What is the absorbed dose in Gy?

\[\text{Dose} = \frac{0.15}{0.05} = \boxed{3.0 \text{ Gy}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: From Activity to Dose

A 100g tissue sample absorbs 10‚Å∏ gamma ray photons, each with energy 1.5 MeV. Calculate the absorbed dose in Gy.

(1 MeV = 1.6 √ó 10‚Åª¬π¬≥ J)

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Complete Problem

A radioactive source with activity 5 mCi emits gamma rays for 10 minutes. Each decay releases 0.8 MeV of energy, and 20% is absorbed by a 200g organ. Calculate the absorbed dose.

Step 1: Number of decays.

\[N = R \times t = (5 \times 10^{-3} \times 3.7 \times 10^{10}) \times (10 \times 60)\]

\[N = 1.85 \times 10^8 \times 600 = 1.11 \times 10^{11} \text{ decays}\]

\[E = 0.20 \times 1.11 \times 10^{11} \times 0.8 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-13}\]

\[E = 2.84 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J}\]

Step 3: Absorbed dose.

\[\text{Dose} = \frac{2.84 \times 10^{-3}}{0.2} = \boxed{0.0142 \text{ Gy} = 14.2 \text{ mGy}}\]

‚ò¢Ô∏è Topic B: Effective Dose & RBE

Core Formula: \(\text{Effective Dose (Sv)} = \text{Absorbed Dose (Gy)} \times \text{RBE}\)

‚≠ê Level 1: Basic Calculation

A worker receives 0.1 Gy of gamma radiation (RBE = 1). What is the effective dose?

\[\text{Effective Dose} = 0.1 \times 1 = \boxed{0.1 \text{ Sv} = 100 \text{ mSv}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: Alpha Radiation

A tissue receives 5 mGy of alpha radiation (RBE = 20). Calculate the effective dose in mSv.

\[\text{Effective Dose} = 5 \times 20 = \boxed{100 \text{ mSv}}\]

Note: 5 mGy of alpha is as harmful as 100 mGy of gamma!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Mixed Radiation

A person receives 10 mGy from gamma rays (RBE=1) and 2 mGy from neutrons (RBE=10). What is the total effective dose?

Gamma contribution: \(10 \times 1 = 10\) mSv

Neutron contribution: \(2 \times 10 = 20\) mSv

\[\text{Total} = 10 + 20 = \boxed{30 \text{ mSv}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Annual Limit Check

A radiation worker receives 20 mGy/month of X-rays (RBE=1). Is this within the annual limit of 50 mSv?

Monthly effective dose: \(20 \times 1 = 20\) mSv

Annual dose: \(20 \times 12 = 240\) mSv

\[\boxed{\text{240 mSv} > \text{50 mSv limit ‚Äî NOT safe!}}\]

The worker exceeds the limit by almost 5 times!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Comparing Dangers

Person A receives 100 mGy of gamma rays. Person B receives 10 mGy of alpha particles (RBE=20). Who received more biologically damaging radiation?

Person A (gamma): \(100 \times 1 = 100\) mSv

Person B (alpha): \(10 \times 20 = 200\) mSv

\[\boxed{\text{Person B received more damage (200 mSv vs 100 mSv)}}\]

Despite receiving 10√ó less energy, the alpha radiation is twice as harmful!

‚ö†Ô∏è Topic D: Radiation Safety Calculations

‚≠ê Level 1: Lifetime Dose

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Time to Fatal Dose

A radiation source produces 100 mSv/hour at 1 meter distance. How long until someone reaches the LD50 of 4 Sv?

\[t = \frac{4000 \text{ mSv}}{100 \text{ mSv/hr}} = \boxed{40 \text{ hours}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Distance Effect

The dose rate at 1m from a source is 200 mSv/hr. Using the inverse square law, what is the dose rate at 4m?

Inverse Square Law: \(I \propto 1/r^2\)

\[\text{Rate at 4m} = 200 \times \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 = 200 \times \frac{1}{16} = \boxed{12.5 \text{ mSv/hr}}\]

Quadrupling distance reduces exposure by 16√ó!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Emergency Scenario

After a nuclear accident, a worker must perform a 5-minute emergency task at a location with dose rate 1 Sv/hr. What effective dose will they receive, and what percentage of the LD50 is this?

Step 1: Calculate dose.

\[\text{Dose} = 1 \text{ Sv/hr} \times \frac{5}{60} \text{ hr} = \boxed{0.083 \text{ Sv} = 83 \text{ mSv}}\]

Step 2: Percentage of LD50 (4 Sv).

\[\frac{0.083}{4} \times 100 = \boxed{2.1\%}\]

This single exposure exceeds the annual limit (50 mSv) but is not immediately dangerous.
