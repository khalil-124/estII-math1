1. Structure and Properties of the Nucleus

The nucleus is the dense core of an atom, composed of nucleons:

  • Protons (\(p\)): Positively charged. Mass \(m_p \approx 1.67262 \times 10^{-27}\) kg.
  • Neutrons (\(n\)): Neutral (no charge). Slightly heavier than protons. Mass \(m_n \approx 1.67493 \times 10^{-27}\) kg.

Particle Mass Comparison

Particle Symbol Mass (kg) Mass (u) Charge
Proton \(p\) \(1.67262 \times 10^{-27}\) 1.007276 +e
Neutron \(n\) \(1.67493 \times 10^{-27}\) 1.008665 0
Electron \(e^-\) \(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\) 0.000549 -e

Note: The electron is approximately 1836 times lighter than a proton!

Key Notation: A nuclide is represented as:
\({}_{Z}^{A}X\)
  • \(X\): Chemical symbol of the element.
  • \(Z\) (Atomic Number): Number of protons. Determines the element.
  • \(A\) (Atomic Mass Number): Total number of nucleons (\(A = Z + N\)).
  • \(N\) (Neutron Number): \(N = A - Z\).

Isotopes

Nuclei with the same \(Z\) (same element) but different \(N\) (and thus different \(A\)).

Example: Carbon-12 (\({}^{12}C\)) and Carbon-14 (\({}^{14}C\)).

Natural Abundance: The percentage of a specific isotope found naturally on Earth.

‚úì Quick Check: Two isotopes of the same element have:

Answer: A

Nuclear Radius

The nucleus is roughly spherical. Its radius \(r\) depends on the mass number \(A\):

\[r \approx r_0 \cdot A^{1/3}\] Where \(r_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\) m = 1.2 fm (femtometers).

‚úì Quick Check: If nucleus A has \(A = 27\) and nucleus B has \(A = 64\), the ratio \(r_A / r_B\) is:

A) 27/64   B) 3/4   C) 9/16   D) 1/2

Answer: B (\(27^{1/3}/64^{1/3} = 3/4\))

Atomic Mass Units (u)

Masses are often measured in unified atomic mass units (u), based on Carbon-12 being exactly 12.0000 u.

\[1 \text{ u} = 1.6605 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} = 931.5 \text{ MeV}/c^2\]

Mass-Energy Equivalence: \(E = mc^2\). The rest energy of 1 u is 931.5 MeV.

2. Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of radiation by an unstable nucleus.

Historical Discovery

Towards the end of the 19th century, scientists discovered that certain minerals could darken photographic plates even in the absence of light. This phenomenon is now called radioactivity.

Marie and Pierre Curie isolated two new highly radioactive elements: Polonium (Po) and Radium (Ra). Marie Curie won two Nobel Prizes for this groundbreaking work.

Types of Radiation

Type Symbol Identity Penetrating Power
Alpha \(\alpha\) Helium Nucleus (\({}_{2}^{4}He\)) Low (stopped by paper)
Beta \(\beta\) Electron (\(e^-\)) or Positron (\(e^+\)) Medium (stopped by 3mm Al)
Gamma \(\gamma\) High energy photon High (stopped by cm of Lead)

3. Decay Laws and Half-Life

Radioactive decay is a random, probabilistic process.

Decay Rate (Activity)

The rate at which nuclei decay is proportional to the number of nuclei present:

\[\frac{\Delta N}{\Delta t} = -\lambda N\]

\(\lambda\) (Lambda): Decay constant (probability of decay per unit time).

Exponential Decay Law

The number of parent nuclei \(N\) remaining at time \(t\) is:

\[N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}\]

Half-Life (\(T_{1/2}\))

The time required for half of the nuclei in a sample to decay.

\[T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda}\]

‚úì Quick Check: If a sample starts with 800 nuclei, how many remain after 2 half-lives?

A) 400   B) 200   C) 100   D) 50

Answer: B (\(800 \div 2 \div 2 = 200\))

‚úì Quick Check: If \(T_{1/2} = 10\) days, what fraction remains after 30 days?

A) 1/2   B) 1/4   C) 1/8   D) 1/16

Answer: C (3 half-lives ‚Üí \((1/2)^3 = 1/8\))

Mean Life (\(\tau\))

The average lifetime of a nucleus.

\[\tau = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{T_{1/2}}{0.693}\]

‚úì Quick Check: Mean life \(\tau\) is related to decay constant \(\lambda\) by:

A) \(\tau = \lambda\)   B) \(\tau = 1/\lambda\)   C) \(\tau = 0.693\lambda\)   D) \(\tau = \lambda^2\)

Answer: B

Activity (\(R\))

The number of decays per second.

\[R = \left| \frac{\Delta N}{\Delta t} \right| = \lambda N = R_0 e^{-\lambda t}\]

Units:

  • Becquerel (Bq): 1 decay/s (SI Unit).
  • Curie (Ci): \(1 \text{ Ci} = 3.70 \times 10^{10}\) Bq.

4. Radioactive Dating

Carbon-14 Dating: Used for organic materials up to ~60,000 years old. Living things maintain a constant ratio of \({}^{14}C/{}^{12}C\). Upon death, \({}^{14}C\) decays (\(T_{1/2} \approx 5730\) yr) and is not replaced.

Geological Dating: Uses isotopes with very long half-lives (e.g., Uranium-238, \(T_{1/2} = 4.5 \times 10^9\) yr) to date rocks.

(a) What is the decay constant of \({}_{92}^{238}U\) whose half-life is \(4.5 \times 10^9\) yr?

(b) The decay constant of a given nucleus is \(3.2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ s}^{-1}\). What is its half-life?

(b)

Example 30-9 (Slide 17): Activity Calculation

The isotope \({}_{6}^{14}C\) has a half-life of 5730 yr. If a sample contains \(1.00 \times 10^{22}\) carbon-14 nuclei, what is the activity of the sample?

Step 1: Convert half-life to seconds.

\[T_{1/2} = 5730 \text{ yr} \times 3.156 \times 10^7 \text{ s/yr} \approx 1.81 \times 10^{11} \text{ s}\]

Step 2: Calculate decay constant \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{1.81 \times 10^{11}} \approx 3.83 \times 10^{-12} \text{ s}^{-1}\]

Step 3: Calculate Activity \(R = \lambda N\).

\[R = (3.83 \times 10^{-12})(1.00 \times 10^{22}) = \boxed{3.83 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}}\]

(Approx 1.03 Ci)

Question 49 (Slide 17): Finding Half-Life from Activity Drop

The activity of a sample drops by a factor of 6.0 in 9.4 minutes. What is its half-life?

Given: \(\frac{R_0}{R} = 6.0\), \(t = 9.4\) min.

\[\frac{R_0}{R} = e^{\lambda t}\]

\[\ln(6.0) = \lambda t\]

\[\lambda = \frac{1.792}{9.4} \approx 0.1906 \text{ min}^{-1}\]

\[T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{0.1906} \approx \boxed{3.6 \text{ minutes}}\]

Example 30-11 (Slide 19): Nitrogen-13 Decay

A laboratory has 1.49 Œºg of pure \({}_{7}^{13}N\) which has a half-life of 10.0 min (600 s).

  1. How many nuclei are present initially?
  2. What is the rate of decay (activity) initially?
  3. What is the activity after 1.00 h?
  4. After approximately how long will the activity drop to less than 1 decay/s?

(a) Initial Nuclei \(N_0\):

Atomic mass of \({}^{13}N \approx 13\) g/mol. Mass \(m = 1.49 \times 10^{-6}\) g.

Moles = \(\frac{1.49 \times 10^{-6}}{13} \approx 1.146 \times 10^{-7}\) mol.

\[N_0 = (1.146 \times 10^{-7}) \times (6.022 \times 10^{23}) \approx \boxed{6.90 \times 10^{16} \text{ nuclei}}\]

(b) Initial Activity \(R_0\):

\(\lambda = 0.693 / 600 = 1.155 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}^{-1}\)

\[R_0 = \lambda N_0 = (1.155 \times 10^{-3})(6.90 \times 10^{16}) \approx \boxed{7.97 \times 10^{13} \text{ Bq}}\]

(c) Activity after 1 hour (3600 s):

1 hour = 6 half-lives. \(R = R_0 / 2^6 = R_0 / 64\)

\[R \approx \boxed{1.25 \times 10^{12} \text{ Bq}}\]

(d) Time to reach \(R = 1\):

\[t = \frac{\ln(R_0)}{\lambda} = \frac{\ln(7.97 \times 10^{13})}{1.155 \times 10^{-3}} \approx 27700 \text{ s} \approx \boxed{7.7 \text{ hours}}\]

Example 30-13 (Slide 23): Carbon Dating

An ancient animal bone fragment contains 200 g of carbon and registers an activity of 16 decays/s. What is its age?

Step 1: Estimate Initial Activity (\(R_0\)).

Living ratio is approx \(R/m \approx 0.23\) Bq/g.

\[R_0 \approx 0.23 \times 200 = 46 \text{ decays/s}\]

Step 2: Current Activity \(R = 16\) decays/s.

\[e^{\lambda t} = \frac{R_0}{R} = \frac{46}{16} = 2.875\]

\[\lambda t = \ln(2.875) \approx 1.056\]

Step 3: Using \(\lambda = 0.693 / 5730 \approx 1.21 \times 10^{-4} \text{ yr}^{-1}\):

\[t = \frac{1.056}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} \approx \boxed{8730 \text{ years}}\]

Question 42 (Slide 24): Iodine-131 Activity

The iodine isotope \({}^{131}I\) (\(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) days) is used in hospitals. If 782 Œºg are ingested, determine the activity:

  1. Immediately
  2. 1.50 h later
  3. 3.0 months later

Constants: \(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) d = \(6.93 \times 10^5\) s. Atomic Mass ‚âà 131 g/mol.

(a) Initial Activity:

Mass = \(7.82 \times 10^{-4}\) g. Moles = \(\frac{7.82 \times 10^{-4}}{131} = 5.97 \times 10^{-6}\) mol.

\(N_0 = 5.97 \times 10^{-6} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.59 \times 10^{18}\) nuclei.

\(\lambda = 0.693 / (6.93 \times 10^5) \approx 1.00 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s}^{-1}\).

\[R_0 = \lambda N_0 = \boxed{3.59 \times 10^{12} \text{ Bq}}\]

(b) After 1.5 hours: Time is very small compared to half-life.

\[R \approx \boxed{3.57 \times 10^{12} \text{ Bq}}\]

(c) After 3.0 months (~90 days):

Number of half-lives = 90 / 8.02 ‚âà 11.2.

\[R = R_0 (1/2)^{11.2} \approx \boxed{1.5 \times 10^9 \text{ Bq}}\]

Question 43 (Slide 25): Nuclei Count from Activity

How many nuclei of \({}_{92}^{238}U\) remain in a rock if the activity registers 420 decays per second?

Given: \(R = 420\) Bq. \(T_{1/2} = 4.5 \times 10^9\) yr.

Step 1: Convert half-life to seconds.

\(T_{1/2} = 4.5 \times 10^9 \times 3.156 \times 10^7 = 1.42 \times 10^{17}\) s.

Step 2: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\(\lambda = 0.693 / (1.42 \times 10^{17}) \approx 4.88 \times 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1}\).

Step 3: Calculate \(N = R / \lambda\).

\[N = \frac{420}{4.88 \times 10^{-18}} \approx \boxed{8.6 \times 10^{19} \text{ nuclei}}\]

Question 46 (Slide 26): Mass from Activity

Calculate the mass of a sample of pure \({}_{19}^{40}K\) with an initial decay rate of \(2.4 \times 10^5 \text{ s}^{-1}\). The half-life is \(1.248 \times 10^9\) yr.

Step 1: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\(T_{1/2} = 1.248 \times 10^9 \times 3.156 \times 10^7 \approx 3.94 \times 10^{16}\) s.

\(\lambda = 0.693 / (3.94 \times 10^{16}) \approx 1.76 \times 10^{-17} \text{ s}^{-1}\).

Step 2: Calculate Number of Nuclei.

\(N = R / \lambda = (2.4 \times 10^5) / (1.76 \times 10^{-17}) \approx 1.36 \times 10^{22}\) nuclei.

Step 3: Calculate Mass.

Moles = \(N / N_A = (1.36 \times 10^{22}) / (6.022 \times 10^{23}) \approx 0.0226\) mol.

\[Mass = 0.0226 \times 40 \approx \boxed{0.90 \text{ g}}\]

Prove that nuclear density is approximately constant for all nuclei and calculate its value.

Step 3: Density calculation.

\[\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{A \times 1.66 \times 10^{-27}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi r_0^3 A} = \frac{1.66 \times 10^{-27}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi (1.2 \times 10^{-15})^3}\]

\[\rho = \frac{1.66 \times 10^{-27}}{7.24 \times 10^{-45}} \approx \boxed{2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3}\]

‚úì Key insight: The A cancels! Density is independent of A ‚Äî constant for all nuclei!

‚è±Ô∏è Topic C: Half-Life & Decay

Core Formulas:

\[T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}\] \[N = N_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/T_{1/2}} = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}\]
‚≠ê Level 1: One Half-Life

A sample contains 1000 nuclei. How many remain after one half-life?

After each half-life, half the nuclei remain:

\[N = \frac{1000}{2} = \boxed{500 \text{ nuclei}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: Multiple Half-Lives

A sample has a half-life of 4 hours. If it starts with 800 nuclei, how many remain after 12 hours?

Step 1: Number of half-lives.

\[n = \frac{12}{4} = 3\]

Step 2: Calculate remaining nuclei.

\[N = 800 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{800}{8} = \boxed{100 \text{ nuclei}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Finding Half-Life

The decay constant of an isotope is \(\lambda = 0.0231 \text{ day}^{-1}\). What is its half-life?

Formula: \(T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}\)

\[T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{0.0231} = \boxed{30 \text{ days}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Activity Drop

The activity of a sample drops by a factor of 6.0 in 9.4 minutes. What is its half-life?

Given: \(\frac{R_0}{R} = 6\), \(t = 9.4\) min

Step 1: Find \(\lambda\).

\[\frac{R_0}{R} = e^{\lambda t} \Rightarrow \ln(6) = \lambda t\]

\[\lambda = \frac{1.792}{9.4} = 0.1906 \text{ min}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Calculate half-life.

\[T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{0.1906} = \boxed{3.6 \text{ minutes}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Percentage Remaining

A Co-60 sample (\(T_{1/2} = 5.27\) years) has decayed to 35% of its original amount. How much time has passed?

Given: \(\frac{N}{N_0} = 0.35\)

Step 1: Find \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{5.27} = 0.1315 \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Use exponential decay law.

\[0.35 = e^{-\lambda t} \Rightarrow \ln(0.35) = -\lambda t\]

\[-1.05 = -0.1315 \times t\]

Step 3: Solve for t.

\[t = \frac{1.05}{0.1315} = \boxed{7.98 \text{ years}}\]

C-14 has a half-life of 5730 years. If a sample contains \(1 \times 10^{22}\) nuclei, what is its activity in Bq?

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Finding N from Activity

How many Tc-99m nuclei (\(T_{1/2} = 6\) hours) are needed to produce an activity of 1.0 mCi?

Step 2: Calculate \(\lambda\).

Step 3: Calculate N.

\[N = \frac{R}{\lambda} = \frac{3.7 \times 10^{7}}{3.21 \times 10^{-5}} = \boxed{1.15 \times 10^{12} \text{ nuclei}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Ordering Activity

A lab needs I-131 (\(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) days) with activity 50 mCi in 2 days. What activity must be ordered today?

Key Insight: We need \(R = 50\) mCi after \(t = 2\) days. Find \(R_0\) now.

Step 1: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{8.02} = 0.0864 \text{ day}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Reverse the decay formula.

\[R = R_0 e^{-\lambda t} \Rightarrow R_0 = R \cdot e^{\lambda t}\]

\[R_0 = 50 \times e^{(0.0864)(2)} = 50 \times 1.189 = \boxed{59.4 \text{ mCi}}\]

üè• Application A: Nuclear Medicine

Radioactive isotopes are widely used in medical diagnosis and treatment.

‚≠ê Level 1: Thyroid Scan Basics

A hospital uses Iodine-131 (\(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) days) for thyroid scans. If a patient receives a dose with activity 5.0 mCi, what will the activity be after 24 hours?

Given: \(R_0 = 5.0\) mCi, \(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) days, \(t = 1\) day

Step 1: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{8.02} = 0.0864 \text{ day}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Apply decay formula.

\[R = R_0 e^{-\lambda t} = 5.0 \times e^{-0.0864 \times 1}\]

\[R = 5.0 \times 0.917 = \boxed{4.59 \text{ mCi}}\]

Clinical Note: The activity decreases slowly because 1 day is much less than the half-life.

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: PET Scan Isotope

Fluorine-18 is used in PET scans with \(T_{1/2} = 110\) minutes. A hospital receives a shipment of 50 mCi at 6:00 AM. How much activity remains at 9:00 AM when the patient arrives?

Given: \(R_0 = 50\) mCi, \(T_{1/2} = 110\) min, \(t = 180\) min (3 hours)

Step 1: Number of half-lives.

\[n = \frac{180}{110} = 1.636\]

Step 2: Calculate remaining activity.

\[R = R_0 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{1.636} = 50 \times 0.322 = \boxed{16.1 \text{ mCi}}\]

Clinical Note: This is why PET scan isotopes must be produced nearby - they decay quickly!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Cancer Treatment Planning

A cancer patient needs radiation therapy using Cobalt-60 (\(T_{1/2} = 5.27\) years). The treatment requires a minimum activity of 1000 Ci. If the hospital installs a source with 1500 Ci today, for how many years can it be used before replacement?

Given: \(R_0 = 1500\) Ci, \(R_{min} = 1000\) Ci, \(T_{1/2} = 5.27\) yr

Step 1: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{5.27} = 0.1315 \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Find time when \(R = 1000\) Ci.

\[1000 = 1500 \times e^{-0.1315 t}\]

\[\frac{1000}{1500} = e^{-0.1315 t}\]

\[\ln(0.667) = -0.1315 t\]

\[t = \frac{0.405}{0.1315} = \boxed{3.08 \text{ years}}\]

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Technetium-99m Preparation

A nuclear pharmacy needs to prepare a dose of Tc-99m (\(T_{1/2} = 6.0\) hours) with activity 20 mCi at 10:00 AM for a patient. The pharmacy receives the isotope at 6:00 AM. What initial activity should be ordered?

Given: Need \(R = 20\) mCi at \(t = 4\) hours, \(T_{1/2} = 6\) hr

Step 1: Calculate \(\lambda\).

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{6} = 0.1155 \text{ hr}^{-1}\]

Step 2: Work backwards to find \(R_0\).

\[R = R_0 e^{-\lambda t} \Rightarrow R_0 = R \times e^{\lambda t}\]

\[R_0 = 20 \times e^{0.1155 \times 4} = 20 \times 1.588 = \boxed{31.8 \text{ mCi}}\]

The pharmacy must order about 60% more than needed to account for decay!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Radiation Dose Calculation

A patient receives a thyroid treatment with 150 mCi of I-131. The thyroid gland (mass 20g) absorbs 90% of the iodine. Given that each decay releases 0.5 MeV of absorbed energy, calculate the total absorbed dose in Gray (Gy) over the first 24 hours.

(1 Gy = 1 J/kg, 1 MeV = \(1.6 \times 10^{-13}\) J)

Step 1: Activity absorbed by thyroid.

\[R_0 = 0.90 \times 150 = 135 \text{ mCi} = 135 \times 3.7 \times 10^7 = 5.0 \times 10^9 \text{ Bq}\]

Step 2: Total decays in 24 hours.

\(\lambda = 0.693/(8.02 \times 24) = 0.0036 \text{ hr}^{-1}\)

Average activity ‚âà \(R_0 \times 0.958 = 4.79 \times 10^9\) Bq

Total decays = \(4.79 \times 10^9 \times 86400 = 4.14 \times 10^{14}\) decays

Step 3: Energy deposited.

\[E = 4.14 \times 10^{14} \times 0.5 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-13} = 33.1 \text{ J}\]

Step 4: Dose in Gray.

\[D = \frac{E}{m} = \frac{33.1}{0.020} = \boxed{1655 \text{ Gy}}\]

This high dose is intentional for destroying thyroid cancer cells!

üèõÔ∏è Application B: Archaeological Dating

Carbon-14 dating helps archaeologists determine the age of organic artifacts.

‚≠ê Level 1: Dead Sea Scrolls

A fragment from the Dead Sea Scrolls shows C-14 activity of 78% compared to living material. Estimate the age of the scrolls.

Given: \(\frac{R}{R_0} = 0.78\), \(T_{1/2} = 5730\) yr

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{5730} = 1.21 \times 10^{-4} \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

\[0.78 = e^{-\lambda t} \Rightarrow \ln(0.78) = -\lambda t\]

\[t = \frac{0.248}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} = \boxed{2050 \text{ years}}\]

This matches historical records placing them around 100 BCE - 100 CE!

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: Egyptian Mummy

Linen wrappings from an Egyptian mummy have C-14 activity of 8.1 decays/min per gram. Fresh linen has 15.3 decays/min per gram. How old is the mummy?

\[\frac{R}{R_0} = \frac{8.1}{15.3} = 0.529\]

\[0.529 = e^{-\lambda t} \Rightarrow \ln(0.529) = -1.21 \times 10^{-4} \times t\]

\[t = \frac{0.636}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} = \boxed{5260 \text{ years}}\]

This dates to around 3240 BCE - the Old Kingdom period of Egypt!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Prehistoric Cave Painting

Charcoal from a prehistoric cave painting contains only 12% of the original C-14. Estimate the age of the painting.

Given: \(\frac{N}{N_0} = 0.12\)

\[0.12 = e^{-\lambda t}\]

\[\ln(0.12) = -1.21 \times 10^{-4} \times t\]

\[t = \frac{2.12}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} = \boxed{17,520 \text{ years}}\]

This is from the Late Paleolithic era, similar to the famous Lascaux cave paintings!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Volcanic Rock Dating

A volcanic rock contains Potassium-40 (\(T_{1/2} = 1.25 \times 10^9\) years) and its decay product Argon-40. Analysis shows the K-40/Ar-40 ratio is 3:1. How old is the rock?

Key Insight: If K-40/Ar-40 = 3:1, then 25% of original K-40 has decayed.

Remaining K-40 = 75% = 0.75 of original.

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{1.25 \times 10^9} = 5.54 \times 10^{-10} \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

\[0.75 = e^{-\lambda t}\]

\[\ln(0.75) = -5.54 \times 10^{-10} \times t\]

\[t = \frac{0.288}{5.54 \times 10^{-10}} = \boxed{5.2 \times 10^8 \text{ years}}\]

About 520 million years old - from the Cambrian period!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Uranium-Lead Dating

A meteorite sample contains uranium and lead isotopes. The ratio of Pb-206 to U-238 is 0.45. Given U-238 has \(T_{1/2} = 4.5 \times 10^9\) years, calculate the age of the meteorite.

Given: Pb-206/U-238 = 0.45

Step 1: Relate to original U-238.

If current U = 1, then original U = 1 + 0.45 = 1.45 (Pb came from decayed U)

\[\frac{N}{N_0} = \frac{1}{1.45} = 0.69\]

Step 2: Calculate age.

\[\lambda = \frac{0.693}{4.5 \times 10^9} = 1.54 \times 10^{-10} \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

\[0.69 = e^{-\lambda t}\]

\[t = \frac{\ln(0.69)}{-1.54 \times 10^{-10}} = \frac{0.371}{1.54 \times 10^{-10}}\]

\[t = \boxed{2.41 \times 10^9 \text{ years}}\]

About 2.4 billion years old - from the Proterozoic eon!

üè≠ Application C: Industrial & Safety Applications

Radioactivity is used in industry for quality control, leak detection, and safety monitoring.

‚≠ê Level 1: Smoke Detector

A smoke detector contains 0.9 ŒºCi of Americium-241 (\(T_{1/2} = 432\) years). How much activity will remain after 50 years?

Given: \(R_0 = 0.9\) ŒºCi, \(T_{1/2} = 432\) yr, \(t = 50\) yr

Number of half-lives: \(n = 50/432 = 0.116\)

\[R = 0.9 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{0.116} = 0.9 \times 0.923 = \boxed{0.83 \text{ ŒºCi}}\]

Smoke detectors remain effective for decades because Am-241 has a long half-life!

‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 2: Pipeline Leak Detection

Engineers inject 2.0 mCi of Na-24 (\(T_{1/2} = 15\) hours) into an oil pipeline to detect leaks. What activity will a leak detector measure after 45 hours if all the tracer has accumulated at the leak point?

Given: \(R_0 = 2.0\) mCi, \(t = 45\) hr = 3 half-lives

\[R = 2.0 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = 2.0 \times 0.125 = \boxed{0.25 \text{ mCi}}\]

The short half-life ensures the radioactivity doesn't persist in the environment!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 3: Nuclear Power Plant Safety

After a reactor shutdown, I-131 (\(T_{1/2} = 8.02\) days) is a major concern. If the initial I-131 activity is 10,000 Ci, how long until it drops below the safety limit of 100 Ci?

Given: \(R_0 = 10000\) Ci, \(R = 100\) Ci

\[\frac{R}{R_0} = \frac{100}{10000} = 0.01\]

\[0.01 = e^{-\lambda t}\]

\[\lambda = 0.693/8.02 = 0.0864 \text{ day}^{-1}\]

\[\ln(0.01) = -0.0864 \times t\]

\[t = \frac{4.605}{0.0864} = \boxed{53.3 \text{ days}}\]

This is about 6.6 half-lives. The contaminated area needs isolation for nearly 2 months!

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 4: Radiation Worker Exposure

A radiation worker is exposed to a Co-60 source at a distance of 1m for 30 minutes. The source activity is 500 mCi. If the exposure rate constant for Co-60 is 1.32 R¬∑cm¬≤/(mCi¬∑hr), what is the worker's exposure in Roentgens?

Given: Distance \(d = 100\) cm, \(R = 500\) mCi, \(t = 0.5\) hr

Exposure rate constant \(\Gamma = 1.32\) R¬∑cm¬≤/(mCi¬∑hr)

Formula: Exposure rate \(= \frac{\Gamma \times A}{d^2}\)

\[Rate = \frac{1.32 \times 500}{100^2} = \frac{660}{10000} = 0.066 \text{ R/hr}\]

Total Exposure:

\[Exposure = 0.066 \times 0.5 = \boxed{0.033 \text{ R} = 33 \text{ mR}}\]

This is below typical occupational limits of 100 mR/week.

‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê Level 5: Nuclear Waste Storage

Cesium-137 (\(T_{1/2} = 30.2\) years) from nuclear waste must decay to 0.1% of its original activity before being considered safe. How long must the waste be stored?

Given: \(\frac{R}{R_0} = 0.001\) (0.1%)

Method 1: Using half-lives

\[\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n = 0.001\]

\[n = \frac{\ln(0.001)}{\ln(0.5)} = \frac{-6.91}{-0.693} = 9.97 \approx 10 \text{ half-lives}\]

\[t = 10 \times 30.2 = \boxed{302 \text{ years}}\]

Method 2: Using \(\lambda\)

\[\lambda = 0.693/30.2 = 0.0229 \text{ yr}^{-1}\]

\[t = \frac{-\ln(0.001)}{0.0229} = \frac{6.91}{0.0229} = 302 \text{ years}\]

Nuclear waste storage facilities must be designed to last for centuries!
